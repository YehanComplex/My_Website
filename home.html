Vision

I am interested in a wide range of subjects, including life and biological complexity, classical and quantum computational theory, economics and econometrics, and social dynamics. I enjoy exploring these areas in depth, while also keeping a broad awareness of fundamental physics—such as gravity and particle physics—to stay connected with current scientific thinking. My goal is to build on the work initiated by pioneers like John von Neumann, Alan Turing, and Gregory Chaitin in computational and complexity theory, and to collaborate with others in sharing new ideas with the world. Over my lifetime, I too aspire to contribute by publishing a series of books and research works. Currently, my work has been conducted independently. I warmly welcome the opportunity to have it refined and edited so that it could potentially be published in professional journals such as arXiv.

At present, my goal is to enter the right master’s program or to secure employment in an environment that allows me to progress further. I specifically plan to pursue a master’s program in a field such as econometrics, while continuing computational theory of life as a personal hobby.

ON THE PHENOMENON OF LIFE

An independent study investigating the nature of life from the perspective of computational theory. The work presents a generalized computational framework and applies it to explain processes such as self-replication, and consciousness. I began this research during the Covid-19 pandemic, motivated by a fear of mortality and a desire to understand, for myself, what life truly is. This study is self-contained, presenting both the main results from research on the origin of life and the computational theory necessary to understand them. While I am modest about the depth of mathematics I was able to explore, I am particularly proud of applying the theories of Turing, von Neumann, and Chaitin to a domain largely overlooked by physicists—an area they often assumed held little interesting mathematics or physics.

ECONOMIC MACHINERY

Economic systems can be challenging to understand due to their cyclic nature, complex interactions, and network effects. In this work, I investigate the machinery of economics—beginning with human drives and progressing to more technical aspects. Time series data is analyzed to reveal underlying cycles, and the use of interest rates as a tool to steer the economy is explored. Additionally, topics such as the global cost-of-living crises and the future of an AI-driven economy are briefly discussed.
